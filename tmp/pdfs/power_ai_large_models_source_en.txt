ABSTRACT In recent years, AI large models, also known as large pre-trained models or foundational models, have achieved remarkable success in various tasks across multiple domains. These models leverage extensive unlabelled datasets from multiple fields and modalities, enabling them to generalize across tasks with minimal labelled data. Their ability has led to advancements in numerous domains However; the application of large models in power systems remains in their early stages, and the potential of large models has not been fully explored. This paper aims to help researchers and engineers grasp the latest advances and trends in large models to foster the development and applications in the power industry It traces the development stages of large models, introduces the concept and architecture of large models, and concludes the verified and remarkable capabilities of the large model Additionally, by integrating existing research, this paper reviews recent advancements and potential appli- cations of large models in power systems, with focus on perception, planning, and control. Moreover; it summarizes the key enhancement technologies for optimizing the effectiveness of large models of power systems Finally, the challenges and risks associated with developing large models including computing power requirements reliability, and safety considerations for power systems are also discussed. Based on the survey, large models for power systems are proven to be a promising paradigm that can improve the efficiency and effectiveness of intelligent power systems, which contributes t0 the reference for the intelligent development of the power industry.

2.1.2
Deep Learning Models
Deep learning models such as CNNs [7], GANs [8], GNNs [9] have been widely applied to various tasks These models auto- matically capture features within the labelled data, improving the accuracy of classification and regression tasks_ However deep learning models require a large amount of labelled data for training to achieve good performance. Manually labelling data is time consuming and labour-intensive and domain-specific data often scarce, limiting the applicability of deep learning models Moreover with limited data, increasing the number of parameters can lead to overfitting, making it difficult for models generalize to other tasks
2.1.3
Pre-Trained Models
To reduce the effort of constructing labelled datasets, fully leverage extensive unprocessed data, and enhance the model's ability to transfer across domains_ researchers proposed using models trained on large-scale datasets to handle different tasks. PTMs offer a two-stage solution based on pre- ~training and fine- tuning: during the pre-training phase, the model learns from large, diverse datasets to learn general features; in the fine- tuning phase, only small amount of labelled data is needed to adapt the models to specific tasks This eliminates the need to train the model from the beginning O spend a large amount of time and effort on data labelling, thereby improving the models generalization capability.
The introduction of the Transformer [10] model in 2017 pro- pelled the development of large models The self-attention mechanism of the Transformer model is used process the input sequence, effectively capturing long-range dependencies and enabling efficient parallel computation, greatly speeding up the training process Large models, represented by GPT [11] and BERT [12], leveraged the advantages of the Transformer archi- tecture through the pre-training and fine-tuning paradigm across numerous downstream tasks and surpassing traditional deep learning models.

Multi-Step Reasoning

Composition of Power Systems
Power systems can be seen as the cooperation of source, network; and load. These components work collaboratively to ensure a stable and efficient supply of electricity, accommoda- ting the balance between supply and demand while integrating various energy sources: Electricity generated from power plants and renewable sources like wind and solar. It flows through high-voltage transmission lines over long distances: Finally; it goes into local distribution networks that deliver it to consumers, such as houses and corporations_
These interactions require sophisticated management In the operational aspects; there are three key processes in the prac- tical operation as shown in Figure 7: perception and forecasting, planning and optimization, operating and control.
Perception and forecasting are study understand current conditions and predict future demand and generation patterns To achieve this, several components are included. Load char- acteristic sensing is a part of understanding current conditions It monitors and analyses the behaviour of energy consumption real time, providing insights into current load conditions. Fault detection and diagnosis are also included, which identifies and addresses any abnormalities failures in the system. The insight of current conditions allows for forecasting future de- mand and generate: Source and load power forecasting use historical and real-time data predict future energy demand and generation patterns:
Planning and optimization strategies are developed to ensure that the network can effectively and safely deliver power from sources to loads_ This process involves several components. Scene generation create data of different operational scenarios; Base on the specific problems, constraints and optimization goals are identified to define the limitations and desired outcomes for the system; The use of optimization solvers and optimization algorithms applies models or tools to find the best solution;, ensuring that the power delivery is both efficient and reliable

Challenges of Large Models for Power Systems This chapter discuss the aspects related to Q3: What are the challenges of using large models power systems? These challenges include the substantial computing power required for training and deployment; the reliability risks stemming from model inaccuracies, and the potential security vulnerabilities posed by using large models in sensitive environments
5.1
Computing Power Requirements
As large models continue to evolve and find increasing appli- cations, the demand for parameters and data required for their training has also expanded significantly. While the capabilities of large models have improved, the complexity of training them and the associated resource requirements have grown propor- tionally After training, large models must be deployed into production environments for practical application: Unlike the training phase, the scale of deployment can be large. Large models often face massive concurrent requests and online in- teractions; leading to significant increase in the demand for servers and processing chips As a result; deployment costs can surpass those incurred during training:
It is important to ensure responsiveness and stability in power systems_ When the requests exceed the model's processing ca - pacity, issues such system unavailability, overload, or slow response times may arise. To address these issues one of the key challenges in deployment is achieving efficient model inference through the integration of distributed architectures and hard- ware acceleration. This requires careful consideration of balancing costs and performance Furthermore, appropriate traffic monitoring and early warning mechanisms are essential for mitigating these risks [101]:
5.2
Reliability Considerations
When generating responses_ large models rely on the distribu- tion of their training data rather than a profound understanding of the real world: Consequently; these models may produce inaccurate or fabricated information, a phenomenon known hallucination The use of sampling algorithms with high un - certainty during the model's decoding phase can also induce

hallucinations in language models [102]. The cause of halluci- nation often lies in the data used for model training: During data collection, if the retrieved information contains inaccura- cies, the model may inadvertently learn incorrect knowledge. Excessive repetition of certain data can also distort the model's memory, leading to biased outputs

Conclusion
The specialized application of large models is steadily pro- gressing, with promising outlook in the power sector. These models are expected to enhance the digitalization and intelli- gence of energy and power systems, improve energy utilization efficiency, and promote the development of renewable energy; leading to smarter and more efficient power operations In this paper; we briefly describe the fundamental architecture of large models based on the Transformer and summarize their core advantages, providing expectations for applications in the power sector: Based on these advantages, we recognize their significant potential at each step of the operation process. In addition; we highlight performance improvement techniques for large models, including fine-tuning, prompt engineering; and rein- forcement learning, to enhance their capabilities in power SyS - tems. Finally; we mention that the use of large models in power systems faces a number of challenges, including computational requirements, hallucination, model biases, and information se- curity concerns: With technological advancements_ future re- searches will address these issues to enhance the practical application large models in the power systems, driving rev- olutionary changes towards a more reliable, efficient; intelligent; and secure power system.
